## Notes

## Florentine Republic, Limited Citizenship, and Patriotism

Machiavelli was born into a political system where his family had long been clients and employees of the Medici. He grew up expecting to work for the Medici and within their orbit of power. Yet the Florentine republic had rules restricting full political participation: families with significant unpaid tax debts could not hold the key elected, lotteried offices. Because Machiavelli’s grandfather had serious unpaid tax liabilities, Machiavelli himself was not fully enfranchised.

Despite this partial exclusion, he devoted his whole life to serving the republic. This is used to illustrate both his intense patriotism and the broader phenomenon that people who could not themselves hold office still deeply loved and cared about the republic. Florentines valued their system as “liberty” because they were ruled by about 5% of citizens instead of a single monarch. By modern standards this is not democracy, but it was an “inch more liberty” than monarchy, and that small increment mattered: people were willing to fight, demonstrate, and wave banners proclaiming *libertas* in its defense. Machiavelli later observes that such emotional investment helped sustain the republican regime.

## Crisis, a Weak Medici, and Machiavelli’s Rise at 29

At a critical moment, a specific Medici—deliberately left unnamed because repeated names cause confusion—came to power very young, roughly 20, and personally weak. He inherited a precarious situation: France was invading Italy, and Florence’s position was unstable. This Medici mishandled diplomacy with France, lost credibility, and fell into disrepute. The Florentine population seized the chance to expel him and re-establish an independent republic.

Machiavelli then served under these post-Medici regimes. In the next phase, a theocratic republic, he was appointed to head diplomacy at only 29, despite coming from a relatively minor branch of the elite. The interviewer underscores how early he was politicized by noting that he had seen his country nearly fall multiple times by age 12, implying that he “grew up fast and scared,” while also bookish and hardworking. These traits help explain how someone so young could be entrusted with such responsibility.

## Wealth, Libraries, and Intellectual Formation

The printing press had existed for about 50 years, but books were still expensive. Machiavelli’s ability to write so extensively is linked to his social position. His family was “minor” only relative to the very top; they were still wealthy, roughly in the top 10%, with previous senators among their ancestors. An analogy is drawn to a 19th‑century family that could send a child to Oxford but was not ducal nobility.

In Florence, elite libraries belonged to powerful families. As a Medici client family, the Machiavellis had access to the Medici library, and his education‑minded family ensured that this bookish child could use such collections. Surviving evidence ties some manuscripts he read or copied to the Medici collection and to other patrician libraries, such as that of the Rucellai. He is known to have visited the Rucellai gardens.

## Enduring Elite Families and Institutional Continuity

The Rucellai and similar families are characterized as “important families” whose influence has persisted astonishingly long. A contemporary example is cited: the Rucellai recently filed a letter with the mayor of Florence opposing a slumlord’s redevelopment plan and advocating turning the land into gardens and art centers, co-signed by other old noble families—the same sort of names that were in the office-lottery bags five centuries ago. These families, including those with Medici blood, still exercise influence even now that Florence is part of the larger Italian republic, demonstrating the long-term staying power of these elite institutions that also shaped Machiavelli’s opportunities and intellectual world.
