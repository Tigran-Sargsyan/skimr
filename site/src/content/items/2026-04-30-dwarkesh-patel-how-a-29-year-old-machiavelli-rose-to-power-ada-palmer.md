## Key Points

- Machiavelli’s family had served the Medici for generations, and he expected to work for them.
- He was legally barred from fully holding lotteried government offices because of his grandfather’s unpaid tax debts.
- Despite partial disenfranchisement, he displayed strong patriotism toward Florence’s republic.
- Florentines valued rule by 5% over a single monarch as a small but vital increase in liberty.
- A young, weak Medici mishandled French diplomacy during an invasion, lost credibility, and was expelled.
- Machiavelli became head of diplomacy at age 29 under the subsequent republican, theocratic regime.
- He had grown up through repeated near-collapses of his country, making him serious, scared, and bookish.
- His wealthy but non-top-tier family and Medici client status gave him access to elite libraries like the Medici and Rucellai collections.

## Notes

## Machiavelli’s Political Context and Family Position

Machiavelli was born into a system dominated by the Medici family, for whom his family had worked for generations. His personal expectation growing up was that he would continue this client relationship and work in government under Medici rule. Florence at this time was not a democracy but an oligarchic republic where roughly 5% of the population effectively ruled, yet this was experienced as a meaningful alternative to monarchy.

Legally, not everyone could participate fully in government. To hold the elected and lotteried offices, one needed to meet certain financial and civic criteria. Machiavelli’s grandfather had accumulated significant unpaid tax debt, which disqualified the family from full enfranchisement. As a result, Machiavelli spent his career working hard for a government in which he was not quite a full citizen.

## Patriotism and the Value of Limited Liberty

This partial exclusion highlights the intensity of his patriotism. Despite being barred from some offices, he was deeply devoted to Florence and its institutions. The broader populace, including those without full formal power, cared passionately about the republic.

Florentines perceived a crucial difference between being ruled by a narrow oligarchic 5% and by a single monarch. From a modern perspective, both arrangements fall short of democracy and the kind of liberty we expect. However, to them, that small “inch more liberty” over monarchy was extraordinarily meaningful. People were willing to fight for it, take to the streets, and wave banners proclaiming “libertas” for their republic. Machiavelli observed that this emotional and civic investment helped sustain the republican system.

## Crisis of Medici Rule and French Invasion

At a critical moment, one Medici ruler came to power very young, around age 20, in charge of a fragile political situation. At the same time, France was invading Italy, creating a severe diplomatic and military crisis. The young Medici was frightened and mishandled the diplomacy with France.

This diplomatic failure caused him to fall into widespread disrepute. The city of Florence seized the opportunity to expel him and end his regime. In the aftermath, Florence reverted to an independent republican government, distinct from Medici control.

## Machiavelli’s Rapid Rise at 29

It was under these subsequent republican regimes, including a theocratic phase, that Machiavelli served. When this new republic needed diplomatic leadership, Machiavelli, at age 29, was placed in charge of diplomacy. This is striking given that he came from a relatively minor family and lacked full formal enfranchisement.

Part of the explanation lies in his accelerated political maturation. By the time he was 12, he had already seen his country nearly fall six times, growing up amidst repeated political and military crises. This produced someone who had “grown up fast,” both frightened by instability and determined to understand and manage politics. He became bookish and intensely hardworking, well prepared for administrative and diplomatic responsibilities despite his youth.

## Social Status, Wealth, and Education

The description of Machiavelli’s background as a “minor” family is relative. His family was not among the very top 5% of Florentine elites, but they were securely within the top 10%. Members of his lineage had served in the Senate, marking them as part of the wealthy elite. In more modern terms, they would be analogous to a family that could send a son to Oxford in the 19th century but did not hold ducal rank.

This status mattered for education and access, especially because books were still expensive even fifty years after the printing press’s invention. Owning many books personally was difficult, but elite networks provided alternatives. In Florence, rich families maintained private libraries, and client relationships enabled access.

## Library Networks and Intellectual Resources

Machiavelli’s family was a Medici client family, granting him access to the Medici library. For a smart, bookish youth being steered toward an intellectual and administrative career, the family would work to ensure use of such collections. Evidence from manuscripts shows that some texts he read or copied were from the Medici collections.

He also accessed other elite libraries, such as that of the Rucellai family. The Rucellai maintained not only a significant library but also gardens that Machiavelli visited, embedding him in a broader intellectual and aristocratic milieu. These resources helped him write extensively despite the underlying expense and scarcity of books.

## Enduring Power of Florentine Elite Families

The Rucellai are given as an example of the extraordinary continuity and staying power of Florentine elite families. They are described as still present and powerful in contemporary Florence. Recently, the Rucellai family filed a letter with the mayor of Florence opposing a slumlord’s redevelopment plans outside the city, advocating instead for gardens and art centers.

This letter was co-signed by about a dozen other old noble families whose names once went into the bags for government lotteries 500 years ago. Many of these lineages intermarried with the Medici, so that Rucellai descendants, for example, have Medici blood. Even though Florence is now part of a larger Italian republic, these families still exert meaningful influence.

This continuity underscores how the institutional and social frameworks that shaped Machiavelli—client networks, elite libraries, and entrenched noble families—have persisted for centuries, linking his world with the present.
