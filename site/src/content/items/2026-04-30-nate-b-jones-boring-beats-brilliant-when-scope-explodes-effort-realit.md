## Notes

## Effort Problems: Simple but Huge

The speaker defines a category of work called “effort problems.” These are not hard because the reasoning is complex but because the overall task is enormous in scope. The difficulty comes from the total volume and surface area, not the intellectual challenge of any one decision or step.

## Concrete Examples of Large-Scale Effort Work

The speaker lists very large tasks that exemplify effort problems:
- Auditing 3,000 vendor contracts to account for compliance changes.
- Migrating a legacy code base containing 2 million lines of COBOL.
- Reviewing every customer interaction from the previous quarter to identify churn signals.

Each of these examples involves thousands or millions of small actions rather than a few highly complex choices.

## Nature of the Cognitive Load

At any single point in these tasks, the required thinking is described as “super straightforward.” Any competent person could handle an individual contract review, a code snippet migration, or analyzing a single customer interaction. The challenge appears when these tasks must be repeated consistently across a vast number of instances.

## The Real Challenge: Sustained Attention and Detail

The core difficulty is maintaining sustained attention and thoroughness across a massive surface area. Over long stretches of repetitive work, people risk losing focus and missing important details. The problem is avoiding dropped details over time, not understanding what to do at each step.

## Agentyc’s Intended Role

The speaker concludes by saying that these are precisely the kinds of problems Agentyc was always built for. The implication is that Agentyc is designed to handle large, repetitive, detail-sensitive workflows. Its purpose is to manage or support the sustained attention and thoroughness required when scope explodes, rather than to solve inherently complex reasoning problems.
