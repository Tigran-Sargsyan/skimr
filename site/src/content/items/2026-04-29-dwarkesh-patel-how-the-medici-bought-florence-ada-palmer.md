## Notes

### Papal banking and pre‑modern tax systems
- In pre‑modern states, central authorities struggled to collect taxes directly, so they delegated collection to local agents.
- A central power would set a quota (e.g., 10,000 florins) that a local collector had to remit; anything collected above that amount the collector kept as income.
- This incentive structure produced “wicked tax collectors” who maximized what they extracted from the population in order to increase their personal gain.
- The papacy operated within this broader logic of delegated collection: money intended for the central authority moved through intermediaries.

### Cosimo de’ Medici as papal banker
- As papal banker, Cosimo became the person who collected and channeled money from every church in Christendom.
- Every coin dropped into collection boxes and offerings made by pilgrims that were due to the papacy effectively flowed first to Cosimo’s hands.
- Cosimo passed these funds on to the papacy after taking a cut, creating a large, rapid flow of money under his control.
- Managing this financial stream not only enriched him but also generated contracts and personal contacts, expanding his influence.

### Manipulating Florence’s lottery government
- Florence used sortition, a lottery-based mechanism, to select governing councils; this system has ancient roots, including in Athens, and can function well but is corruptible.
- Sortition can be subverted much as voting can be: by bribing those who draw names or manipulating the surrounding processes.
- Cosimo’s initial method was subtler and statistical: he became such a major employer that roughly a third of Florence’s population was on his payroll.
- When nine council members were drawn at random, probability alone meant about three would likely be his employees.
- Cosimo pre‑instructed his people on desired policies and told them to consult him if uncertain, so that when a random council lacked a clear majority, his organized plurality could steer decisions.
- Through this tactic, the Medici effectively controlled the city while appearing simply as private citizens benefiting from a neutral lottery.

### Exile, bribery, and consolidation
- In 1430 and 1432, random draws went against Cosimo; many opponents and no allies were selected, and they immediately branded him a traitor.
- He was arrested and locked in a tower, but he secured escape by bribing the guard at his cell with the equivalent of about $300,000 and the captain of the guard with about $700,000.
- Cosimo later wrote that these were the most foolish men he had met, since as Cosimo de’ Medici he would have paid tens of millions to be freed, but they failed to demand more.
- In the next election cycle, the lottery produced officials entirely favorable to Cosimo, who invited him back to Florence in triumph and titled him “father of the fatherland.”
- His returned faction then arrested and persecuted his enemies, who were found guilty of offenses such as tax evasion.
- This turning point led Cosimo to shift strategy: he moved from merely ensuring a favorable plurality to bribing those who actually ran the elections.
- He summarized his view of the relationship between wealth and power in the maxim: “It is dangerous to be rich and not powerful.”
