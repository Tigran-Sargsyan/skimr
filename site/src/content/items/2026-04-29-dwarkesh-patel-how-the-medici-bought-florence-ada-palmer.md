## Key Points

- Pre-modern tax systems used delegated local collectors who remitted a fixed quota and kept any surplus.
- This delegation made central powers heavily dependent on local tax collectors’ loyalty and effectiveness.
- As papal banker, Cosimo de’ Medici routed church income across Christendom through his hands and took a cut.
- Handling papal finances gave Cosimo immense capital, rapid cash flow, and extensive political and commercial connections.
- Florence’s government used sortition, randomly drawing officeholders from a bag, a system that can be corrupted.
- Cosimo informally captured the lottery system by employing a large fraction of citizens, ensuring many drawn officials were beholden to him.
- In 1430–1432 Cosimo’s faction briefly lost the lottery, leading to his arrest and imprisonment as a traitor.
- Cosimo escaped via heavy bribes, then returned to Florence, punished enemies, and decided to bribe election-runners directly, insisting it is dangerous to be rich but not powerful.

## Notes

## Pre-modern Tax Collection and Local Intermediaries

In the pre-modern world, tax collection was logistically difficult and administratively complex. Central authorities did not directly gather taxes from every individual; instead, they delegated collection to local intermediaries. A town would have a local tax collector responsible for going door-to-door, gathering taxes, and forwarding a set amount to the central power. The central authority specified a quota, for example 10,000 florins from a given area, which had to be remitted. The tax collector’s income came from any surplus he could squeeze out above that quota. If he extracted 15,000 florins, he sent 10,000 to the center and kept 5,000 as his pay. This incentivized aggressive extraction and explains why tax collectors gained a reputation for wickedness. The system could be further layered, with even more local collectors under a regional one, deepening dependence on the loyalty and behavior of these delegates.

## Cosimo de’ Medici as Papal Banker

Within this framework of intermediated finance, Cosimo de’ Medici served as banker for the papacy. Being papal banker meant that money intended for the papacy from across Christendom flowed first through Cosimo. When people put coins in church collection boxes or when pilgrims donated at shrines, the funds that should financially support the papacy were routed into Cosimo’s financial network. He then passed the money on to the papacy after taking a cut for himself and his bank. This role involved enormous volumes of capital moving quickly, providing large profits from the float and related financial operations. It also created abundant opportunities to make contracts and cultivate contacts across regions and institutions. The position turned Cosimo from a successful banker into someone whose financial reach and influence extended through the core religious and political structures of Europe.

## From Financial Power to Political Control in Florence

Cosimo leveraged his wealth and networks to dominate his home city, Florence. Florence used sortition, or lottery-based selection, for many political offices, drawing names from a bag. This ancient method, used in places like classical Athens, can function well but is still vulnerable to corruption. Just as voting can be manipulated by bribery, rigged machines, or pressure on voters, sortition can be influenced by bribing those who manage the drawing or by shaping who is in the pool of eligible names. Cosimo’s initial method was indirect: he employed a huge portion of the city’s population. If roughly a third of citizens were on his payroll, then when a small council of nine officials was randomly selected, statistical chance meant about three would often be his employees. Cosimo could instruct his employees in advance about policies he wanted and tell them to consult him if uncertain. When a plurality of randomly chosen councilors already shared his prepared agenda, he effectively controlled policy without overtly holding office. Thus, he captured the lottery system not by directly tampering with the draw, but by ensuring that enough drawn citizens were beholden to him.

## The Setback: Exile and Escape

However, this probabilistic control was not foolproof. Around 1430–1432, Cosimo experienced a period of bad luck in the lotteries. The drawings produced many officials hostile to him and few or none from his faction. Once a council dominated by his enemies emerged, they quickly declared him a traitor to the state. Cosimo was arrested and confined in a tower, suddenly stripped of his de facto control. In prison, he relied on the same resource that had elevated him: money. He bribed his way out, offering the equivalent of about $300,000 to the guard outside his cell and about $700,000 to the captain of the guard to smuggle him out. Later he wrote that these two men were the most foolish he had ever met, because as Cosimo de’ Medici he would gladly have paid tens of millions to secure his liberty, but they lacked the ambition to demand more than hundreds of thousands.

## Return, Consolidation, and Direct Electoral Corruption

After his escape, the next round of elections in Florence fortuitously produced officials overwhelmingly favorable to Cosimo. This new leadership invited him back to the city in triumph, restoring him not as a marginal player but as a celebrated figure. They granted him the honorific “father of the fatherland,” signaling his elevated status in the polity. His enemies, by contrast, were arrested and persecuted, and were found guilty of offenses such as tax evasion and other abuses. This episode marked a turning point in his political strategy. Cosimo decided to move beyond merely ensuring a supportive plurality through employment networks. Instead, he resolved to bribe the people who directly managed and ran the elections and lotteries themselves, shifting from probabilistic influence to tighter control of the mechanisms. His guiding maxim from this experience was that “It is dangerous to be rich and not powerful,” encapsulating his conclusion that wealth without direct political power leaves one vulnerable to hostile forces who can use institutions against you.
